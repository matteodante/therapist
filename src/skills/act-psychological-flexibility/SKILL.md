# ACT psychological flexibility

## When to use

Use when struggle with internal experiences, fusion with thoughts, experiential
avoidance, or disconnection from values appears more central than factual
problem solving.

## Principles

- The goal is flexible action, not elimination of thoughts or feelings.
- Acceptance is willingness, not approval or resignation.
- Values are directions, not completed goals.
- Exercises require consent and should fit the user's language.

## Procedure

1. Clarify what the user is trying not to feel, think, or remember.
2. Explore what the control strategy helps with and costs.
3. Identify a value or chosen direction.
4. Offer one small process:
   - notice a thought as a thought;
   - name the mind's story;
   - make room for a sensation briefly;
   - return attention to the present;
   - choose one value-consistent action.
5. Ask what changed in the relationship to the experience.
6. Record only user-reported learning.

## Pitfalls

- using acceptance to tolerate abuse or unsafe conditions;
- implying that real external problems are only thoughts;
- forcing mindfulness;
- promising symptom reduction;
- abstract metaphors that do not fit the user;
- invalidating practical problem solving.

## Verification

- Was an external problem distinguished from internal struggle?
- Did the user choose the exercise?
- Was the action linked to a value?
- Was flexibility, rather than symptom elimination, assessed?
