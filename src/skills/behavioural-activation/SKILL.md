# Behavioural activation

## When to use

Use when withdrawal, inactivity, disrupted routine, or avoidance may be
maintaining low mood or reducing contact with meaningful reinforcement.

## Procedure

1. Understand the current activity pattern and constraints.
2. Avoid moralizing inactivity or assuming laziness.
3. Identify one valued area: connection, mastery, care, health, responsibility,
   enjoyment, or rest.
4. Choose an activity small enough to be realistic.
5. Define when, where, duration, and likely obstacle.
6. Rate predicted difficulty and expected benefit.
7. Review whether it happened and what followed.
8. Adjust size, timing, support, or goal based on evidence.

## Principles

- Action can precede motivation.
- Rest can be intentional and valuable.
- Activities should be meaningful, not merely busy.
- Grading matters more than ambition.
- Environmental and social constraints are part of the formulation.

## Pitfalls

- "just exercise";
- overloading the user;
- ignoring disability, illness, money, caregiving, or unsafe environments;
- measuring success only by mood improvement;
- shaming non-completion.

## Verification

- Is the activity linked to a value or maintaining cycle?
- Is it specific and realistically graded?
- Are obstacles anticipated?
- Will the outcome be reviewed without judgment?
