# Rumination and worry

## When to use

Use when repetitive thinking is prolonged, circular, difficult to disengage
from, and not producing a concrete decision or action.

## Distinctions

Explore whether the thinking is:

- solving a current controllable problem;
- preparing for a specific action;
- reviewing without new information;
- seeking certainty that cannot be obtained;
- repeatedly judging the self or past;
- avoiding an emotion, decision, or activity.

## Procedure

1. Validate why the mind is trying to solve or prevent something.
2. Ask what answer or certainty the user is trying to obtain.
3. Check whether the thinking has produced new information or action.
4. Identify triggers and short-term function.
5. Separate controllable problems from hypothetical uncertainty.
6. For controllable problems, use problem solving.
7. For repetitive hypothetical thinking, choose a small experiment:
   - postpone worry to a defined period;
   - write one actionable next step;
   - shift attention deliberately;
   - practice observing the thought without continuing the debate.
8. Review effect and feasibility.

## Pitfalls

- telling the user to "stop thinking";
- debating every feared possibility;
- reassurance loops;
- using distraction as permanent avoidance;
- presenting mindfulness as mandatory;
- treating all reflection as pathological.

## Verification

- Did we distinguish productive and repetitive thinking?
- Is the proposed response linked to function?
- Is there one observable experiment?
- Did we plan to review the result?
